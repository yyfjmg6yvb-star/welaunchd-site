How to Get More Google Reviews for Your Business (And Why They Affect Your Ranking)

Chris · June 2026 · 9 min read

Google reviews directly affect where your business ranks in local search results. Review signals — the combination of your total review count, your star rating, how recent your reviews are, and how often you respond — account for 16 to 20% of your Google Local Pack ranking weight. They're also the single factor most businesses neglect. This article explains exactly how to build a steady stream of genuine reviews, how Google uses them, and how the same reviews now feed AI tools like ChatGPT when someone asks for a local business recommendation.

Why Google reviews affect your local search ranking

Google's local ranking algorithm weighs signals across three main categories: relevance (does your business match the search query), distance (how close are you to the searcher), and prominence (how well-known and trusted is your business online). Reviews are the primary driver of prominence.

But Google doesn't just look at your star average. It analyses the entire review signal stack:

  • Volume — total number of reviews across your lifetime
  • Velocity — how recently and how frequently new reviews arrive
  • Rating — your average star rating, weighted toward recent reviews
  • Response rate — what percentage of reviews you've responded to
  • Keyword content — the words reviewers use, which Google indexes as content about your business

A business with 200 reviews from three years ago and nothing recent is algorithmically weaker than a competitor with 60 reviews but a dozen in the last 90 days. Google is assessing current trust, not historical trust.

How do AI tools like ChatGPT use Google reviews?

This is where reviews get even more interesting in 2026. When ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Google's AI Overviews recommend a local business, they're not just looking at your star rating. They're reading the content of your reviews to understand what your business is actually known for.

A business with reviews that repeatedly mention "fast response", "arrived on time", and "fair pricing" will appear in AI results for searches like "reliable plumber who doesn't overcharge" — even though those exact words may not appear anywhere on the business's website. The reviews are effectively a crowd-sourced description of what you're like to work with, and AI reads them as such.

Reviews aren't just a trust signal. In 2026, they're indexed content that describes your business to AI — in the words of real customers who've experienced it.

This means the quality of what reviewers write matters as much as how many reviews you have. A detailed review that mentions the job type, location, and outcome is worth more than five one-star emoji responses to "5 stars!"

How many Google reviews do you actually need?

The benchmark for Local Pack top-3 varies by category and city, but as a general guide:

  • Low competition (rural or niche trade) — 15–30 reviews, 4.0+ average is often enough to appear
  • Medium competition (mid-sized town, common trade) — 40–80 reviews, 4.3+ average
  • High competition (major city, crowded category) — 100+ reviews, 4.4+ average, recent velocity

The honest answer is: look at the businesses currently in positions 1–3 for your target search. That's your actual benchmark. If position 1 has 240 reviews and you have 12, that's the gap you're closing. If position 1 has 38 reviews, you're much closer than you think.

How to ask for Google reviews — the right way

The #1 reason businesses don't have enough reviews is simple: they don't ask. The #2 reason is they ask badly — an email three days after the job that most customers ignore. Here's what actually works:

The verbal ask at the moment of completion

The single most effective method. As you finish the job or hand over the product, say directly: "Everything OK? We'd really appreciate it if you could leave us a Google review — it takes two minutes and really helps us." The customer is at peak satisfaction, you're right there, and there's no awkward follow-up email to ignore.

The WhatsApp or text follow-up

Send a message within 2 hours of completing the job: "Thanks for having us today! If you're happy with everything, a Google review would mean the world to us — here's the link: [direct review link]." The key is the direct link. Don't send them to your website. Don't ask them to search your business name. Remove every possible step between them and the review box.

How to get your direct Google review link

  1. Go to your Google Business Profile dashboard at business.google.com
  2. Click "Ask for reviews" in the right-hand panel
  3. Google generates a short link you can copy
  4. Shorten it further with bit.ly so it's easy to type on mobile
  5. Save this link as a message template on your phone so you're never hunting for it
Key insight

Every step you add between "ask" and "review submitted" reduces conversion. A direct link converts 3–4× better than asking someone to search your business name and find the reviews tab.

QR code on physical materials

Print a QR code linking directly to your review page and put it on: your invoice, receipt, business card, van door, workshop wall, and checkout counter. Anyone who paid you in person can scan and leave a review on the spot while satisfaction is highest.

Email follow-up (use as a backup, not the primary method)

Email works but converts much lower than the above methods. If you use it, send it within 24 hours — conversion drops by over 60% after 48 hours. Keep the email to three sentences: thank them, explain why reviews matter ("helps other customers find us"), and link directly.

What not to do — practices that can get your listing suspended

  • Incentivising reviews — offering discounts, gifts, or payments for positive reviews violates Google's terms and can result in review removal or listing suspension
  • Review gating — only sending the review link to customers you know are happy while filtering out unhappy ones; this is explicitly against Google's policy
  • Buying reviews — purchased reviews look fake, often are flagged and removed by Google's spam detection, and can trigger a full listing review
  • Writing your own reviews — from your own devices, your staff's accounts, or your family's phones; Google tracks device IDs and IP addresses
  • Bulk requesting from an email list — suddenly receiving 40 reviews in a week from accounts that never reviewed anything before is a clear red flag for Google's spam filter

How to respond to Google reviews — and why it matters for ranking

Response rate is a ranking signal. Google tracks what percentage of your reviews receive a response. Businesses that respond to 100% of reviews outperform those that respond to none, even when total review counts are similar. The reason is simple: Google sees responses as evidence of an active, engaged business — the kind worth recommending to searchers.

Responding to positive reviews

Don't just write "Thanks!" — that's low-value text. Instead, naturally include your service type and location in your response. It reads naturally and adds SEO value:

"Thank you so much for the kind words, James. We're really glad the emergency boiler repair went smoothly — it was a cold one! If you need anything else in Harrogate, don't hesitate to give us a call."

This response naturally contains: the business type (boiler repair), the nature of the call (emergency), and the location (Harrogate). That's indexed content in Google's system.

Responding to negative reviews

Respond within 24 hours. Never argue. Acknowledge the experience, apologise for the inconvenience even if you disagree with their account, and offer to take it offline: "I'm sorry to hear this wasn't the experience we aim for. Please call us on [number] and I'll personally make sure this is resolved."

A well-handled negative review response often impresses prospective customers more than a five-star review. It tells them that if something does go wrong, you'll deal with it like a professional. AI tools have also been found to read review responses when forming opinions about a business's reliability.

The review system that runs on autopilot

The businesses that consistently dominate local search on reviews don't work harder — they've built a simple system that runs automatically:

  1. Verbal ask at job completion (every single time, no exceptions)
  2. WhatsApp message within 2 hours with the direct link saved as a template
  3. QR code on invoices and any physical collateral
  4. Reply to every review within 48 hours — set a phone reminder if needed
  5. Monthly: count your reviews and check your velocity. If it's slowed, remind the team to ask more consistently

If you do this for six months, you'll have more reviews than 90% of your local competitors. Combined with a complete Google Business Profile, it puts you in a strong position to appear in the top three Local Pack results.

Frequently asked questions about Google reviews and local ranking

How many Google reviews do I need to rank locally?
There's no fixed number, but businesses in the top three Local Pack positions typically have 50+ reviews with a 4.3+ average. In competitive categories or major cities, 100+ is often needed. More important than the total count is velocity — getting new reviews consistently each month.
Is it against Google's rules to ask customers for reviews?
No — Google explicitly permits asking customers for reviews. What's prohibited is incentivising reviews (discounts in exchange for positive reviews), creating fake reviews, or discouraging negative ones. Simply asking a satisfied customer is perfectly within Google's terms.
How do I get a direct link to my Google review page?
Go to business.google.com, click "Ask for reviews" in the right panel, and Google generates a short direct link. Shorten it with bit.ly for texts and WhatsApp. Save it as a message template on your phone.
How should I respond to negative Google reviews?
Respond within 24 hours, stay calm, acknowledge the experience, and offer to resolve it offline. Never argue publicly. A professional response to a negative review often impresses prospective customers more than the positive reviews themselves.
Do Google reviews affect ChatGPT and AI recommendations?
Yes. ChatGPT uses Google reviews in 58% of local business recommendations, and Perplexity references review data in 100% of local queries. AI reads the content of reviews — not just the star rating — to understand what a business is known for. Recent, descriptive, responded-to reviews are far more valuable than a static rating.
