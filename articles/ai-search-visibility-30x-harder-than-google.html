Getting Found on AI

AI Search Visibility Is 30x Harder Than Google — Here's What to Do About It

Chris · June 2026 · 6 min read

Getting your business found on Google is hard. Getting it found on AI search is up to 30 times harder — that's not a figure I made up, it's from a 2026 study comparing AI visibility to traditional local search results. The good news: the businesses cracking it aren't doing anything technical or expensive. They're just doing the basics really well. Here's what that actually means in practice.

Why AI visibility is so much harder than Google

Think of Google like a library catalogue — it lists thousands of options and lets the reader pick. AI search is more like asking a well-informed friend for a recommendation. Your friend doesn't say "here are 47 plumbers near you." They say "honestly, try Dave — I've heard good things from a few people." The shortlist is short. Usually three to five businesses, sometimes fewer.

That's why the numbers look so brutal. ChatGPT recommends just 1.2% of local businesses when asked for a recommendation. Google's local 3-pack shows up for 35.9% of businesses. AI provides direct, curated answers — and if you're not one of the chosen few, you're completely invisible. Not on page two. Not buried. Gone.

"AI provides direct, curated answers typically mentioning 3–5 brands. If you're not one of them, you're invisible."

The number that changes everything: 45%

In 2025, around 6% of people used AI tools for local search — looking for a restaurant, finding a tradesperson, checking who fixes boilers in their area. By 2026 that number is 45%. A 7.5x increase in a single year.

That means nearly half of all local searches are now going through AI first. If your business isn't showing up in those AI answers, you're missing half the people who might have called you. Not half your Google traffic — half of all the people actively looking for what you do.

The key figure

Local AI search usage jumped from 6% to 45% between 2025 and 2026. Nearly half of local searches now go through AI before Google. Businesses not visible on AI are missing roughly half their potential enquiries.

The silver lining: AI traffic converts brilliantly

Here's the part nobody talks about. Traffic that comes from AI referrals — someone reads an AI recommendation and clicks through to your site — converts at 14.2%. For comparison, organic Google traffic converts at around 2.8%. AI sends you five times fewer people, but those people are five times more likely to become customers.

This makes sense when you think about it. If AI has already told someone "this is the business I'd recommend for what you need," they arrive pre-sold. They're not comparing options — they're checking you out before picking up the phone.

What AI actually looks at when deciding who to recommend

This is the bit most business owners want to skip to, so here it is. AI search engines trust businesses that show up consistently across multiple sources. Think of it like a reputation check: AI is scanning for corroborating evidence before it stakes its reputation on recommending you.

The signals that matter most, in rough order of importance:

  • A complete, active Google Business Profile — still the single most important local trust signal in 2026
  • Consistent NAP (Name, Address, Phone) across all directories — mismatches read as two different businesses
  • Recent reviews that mention specific services — "fixed our boiler same day" beats "5 stars great service"
  • A website that clearly describes what you do, where, and for whom — not a brochure, a clear answer to the question
  • Citations in credible third-party sources — local press, industry directories, editorial mentions
  • Structured data (schema markup) on your website — this is how AI reads your business data without guessing

The structured data thing — don't skip this

Schema markup sounds technical, and it is, a little. But the concept is simple. Right now, AI has to guess what your website is about by reading it like a human would. Schema is a way of adding invisible labels to your pages that say "this is a LocalBusiness, this is our phone number, this is our service area, this is our opening hours." AI can read those labels instantly and reliably.

Websites with proper schema markup are 2.5 times more likely to be cited in AI-generated answers. That's not marginal — that's significant. And most local business websites have none at all.

If you're using a proper website builder or working with someone who knows what they're doing, schema can be added in an afternoon. If your current website doesn't have it, that's a gap worth fixing sooner rather than later.

Unstructured citations: the thing that's actually differentiating businesses right now

Formal directory listings — Google Business Profile, Yelp, Bing Places — are now table stakes. Every serious local business has them. They matter, but they no longer set you apart.

What differentiates in 2026 is unstructured citations: organic editorial mentions. A feature in a local news site. A mention in an industry blog's "best of" roundup. A quote in a podcast transcript. A recommendation in a community forum. These feel harder to get, but they're more powerful — and they're what AI trusts most when deciding whether to recommend you over your competitor who has the same directory listings.

The practical approach: focus first on the core directory listings (get them right and consistent), then look for one or two editorial mention opportunities per month. A quote for a local article, a contribution to an industry newsletter, a response on a relevant forum. Small things. Compounding effect.

Where to start if you're starting from scratch

Don't try to do everything at once. The businesses that gain AI visibility fastest are the ones that do each step properly rather than all steps half-heartedly. Here's the sequence that works:

  1. Claim and complete your Google Business Profile — every field, recent photos, category as specific as possible
  2. Get your NAP consistent — same business name, address, phone on every platform. Exactly the same.
  3. Build 10–15 solid directory listings — Yelp, Bing Places, Apple Maps, and three or four industry-specific ones
  4. Get a proper website — one that clearly describes what you do and has schema markup built in
  5. Ask for specific reviews — prompt customers to mention what you actually did, not just leave a star rating
  6. Add one editorial mention per month — local press, industry blog, community forum. Anything reputable.
Bottom line

AI visibility is harder than Google visibility, but it's not random. Businesses that show up consistently across multiple credible sources, have complete and accurate business information, and earn specific mentions in real editorial content are the ones getting recommended. The gap between those businesses and everyone else is growing every week.
