Google & GBP

How to Get Into Google's Local Pack in 2026 (5-Step Process)

Chris · June 2026 · 8 min read

Google's Local Pack — the block of three businesses that appears at the top of results for searches like "plumber in Leeds" or "accountant near me" — is the most valuable piece of digital real estate a local business can occupy. Getting in requires scoring well across five ranking categories: Google Business Profile completeness, review signals, on-page website SEO, citation consistency, and proximity. This article covers all five in order of impact, so you know exactly where to spend your time.

What is the Google Local Pack and why does it matter so much?

The Local Pack sits above all organic website results for any search with local intent. Someone searching "emergency plumber Birmingham" sees a map and three business cards before they ever reach a single website. Click-through rates for Local Pack positions are dramatically higher than organic results — the top pack position alone captures more traffic than the first organic result.

In 2026, the Local Pack is also the primary source of data that AI tools use to answer local business questions. When ChatGPT recommends a local business, it's pulling heavily from GBP data and the same citation signals that determine Local Pack ranking. Getting into the pack doesn't just win you Google visibility — it feeds your AI search visibility at the same time.

Step 1: Fully complete and optimise your Google Business Profile (32% of ranking)

Your GBP is the single biggest lever you have. Google Business Profile signals account for approximately 32% of your total local ranking weight. The most impactful individual element is your primary business category — it determines which searches you're even eligible to appear for. Choose wrong or leave it too broad, and you're invisible for your most valuable queries.

What to check and fix right now

  • Primary category — be as specific as possible. "Boiler Repair Service" beats "Plumber" for relevant searches. Look at what top-ranking competitors use.
  • Business name — use your exact trading name only. No keyword stuffing. Google will suspend listings that add location or service keywords to the business name.
  • Service area vs storefront — if you travel to customers, set service areas rather than displaying a home address. If you have a premises customers visit, verify your address.
  • Business description — 750 characters max, but front-load the important stuff. Mention your main service and primary location in the first sentence. This text gets read by AI tools.
  • Services section — add every specific service with its own description. This is keyword-rich content Google reads for relevance.
  • Photos — businesses with photos receive 42% more requests for directions. Upload real images: your premises, your team, your work. Add new photos at least once a fortnight.
  • GBP Posts — post at least weekly. Posts signal activity. Profiles that go 30+ days without activity lose freshness signals.
Quick win

Complete profiles earn 7× more clicks than incomplete ones, and 75% of businesses in top-3 Local Pack positions have fully completed their GBP description. If yours is half-finished, fixing it is the fastest move you can make.

Step 2: Build your review signals (16–20% of ranking)

Reviews are the second biggest ranking factor, accounting for 16–20% of your local pack position. What Google weighs isn't just your star rating — it's the combination of total review count, review velocity (how often you're getting new ones), and your response rate.

The benchmark for top-3 in most local markets is 50+ reviews with a 4.3+ average. In high-competition categories or major UK cities, you'll typically need 100+ to compete. More importantly, a steady stream of recent reviews outperforms a large but ageing pile — ten reviews this month are worth more than fifty from two years ago.

How to get more Google reviews consistently

  1. Ask every satisfied customer at the moment of completion — "Would you mind leaving us a Google review? It really helps." The verbal ask converts far better than an email 48 hours later.
  2. Send a follow-up text or WhatsApp with a direct link to your GBP review form. Remove every possible step between the customer and the review box.
  3. Add a QR code linking to your review page on your receipts, business cards, and van.
  4. Reply to every review — positive and negative — within 48 hours. Google tracks your response rate, and responses are indexed content that contributes to your ranking signals.

Response quality matters too. Mention the job type and your location naturally: "Thank you for trusting us with your Birmingham bathroom installation — we're delighted it went smoothly." That's real SEO value in a polite reply.

Step 3: Optimise your website for local signals (19% of ranking)

Your website feeds approximately 19% of your local ranking weight. The most important elements are:

NAP in your footer — identical to your GBP

Your business Name, Address, and Phone number must appear in your website footer in exactly the same format as your Google Business Profile. "123 High Street" on your website and "123 High St" on your GBP are a mismatch — and mismatches reduce Google's confidence in your data. Every page of your site should show your NAP.

Location-specific page content

Your homepage should mention your location in the first paragraph and in at least one H2 heading. If you serve multiple areas, create a dedicated page for each: "Plumber in Leeds", "Plumber in Bradford", "Plumber in Harrogate". These location pages rank for area-specific searches and give Google additional location signals. Vague "serving the whole of Yorkshire" copy doesn't do this — specific town-named pages do.

LocalBusiness schema markup

Schema markup is code that tells Google and AI tools exactly what your business is, where you are, and what you do — in structured, machine-readable format. LocalBusiness schema is one of the most impactful technical additions you can make. Sites with schema markup appear in significantly more AI-generated answers than those without it. If your website was built by WeLaunchd, this is included as standard.

Page speed and mobile experience

Google's local algorithm gives preference to websites that load fast on mobile. Use Google's PageSpeed Insights tool to check your score. Anything below 60 on mobile is hurting you. The most common culprit is oversized images — compress everything before uploading.

Step 4: Build consistent citations across UK directories (7% of ranking)

Citations — mentions of your business name, address, and phone number across directories like Yell, Bing Places, Apple Maps, Yelp, and Foursquare — account for approximately 7% of your local ranking weight. That percentage is smaller than reviews or GBP, but it's foundational. Google uses citation consistency as a verification signal: if 15 different sources all confirm the same business details, Google trusts them.

The crucial rule: your Name, Address, and Phone must be identical across every directory. "Smith & Sons Ltd" and "Smith and Sons" are different in a database. "07700 900123" and "+44 7700 900123" are different formats. Pick your canonical NAP and copy-paste it — never type from memory. Inconsistent citations are one of the main reasons businesses fail ChatGPT's verification check.

The UK directories to prioritise

  • Google Business Profile (primary — this feeds everything else)
  • Bing Places for Business
  • Apple Maps Connect
  • Yelp UK
  • Yell.com
  • Thomson Local
  • Facebook Business
  • Industry-specific: Checkatrade, TrustATrader, Treatwell, TripAdvisor (whichever applies)

Step 5: Understand proximity — and work around it

Proximity — how close your business is to the searcher — accounts for roughly 55% of ranking decisions. This is completely outside your control. What you can do is optimise the other four factors so aggressively that when someone searches from within your natural service area, your business is the clear winner on all the controllable signals.

For mobile searches especially, proximity means someone searching "plumber near me" at their home address will see businesses within a fairly tight radius. This is actually good news for local businesses — national competitors with big budgets can't outspend their way past the proximity filter. Focus on getting factors 1–4 right, and the proximity advantage you already have will do the rest.

Frequently asked questions about Google's Local Pack

What is Google's Local Pack?
Google's Local Pack is the block of three business listings that appears at the top of search results for local queries — for example "plumber in Leeds" or "accountant near me". It includes a map, business name, star rating, address, and phone number. Appearing in the Local Pack puts your business above all organic results.
How long does it take to get into the Google Local Pack?
Most businesses see movement within 4–12 weeks of fully optimising their GBP, building consistent citations, and actively collecting reviews. Highly competitive categories in major cities can take 3–6 months. Lower-competition areas and niche trades often see results faster.
Does my website affect my Local Pack ranking?
Yes — on-page signals account for approximately 19% of your local ranking weight. Your website needs to include your NAP in the footer, use location-specific language, have a dedicated contact page, and ideally include LocalBusiness schema markup.
How many Google reviews do I need to rank in the Local Pack?
Businesses in the top 3 Local Pack positions typically have 50+ reviews with a 4.3+ average. In competitive categories and major cities, 100+ is often needed. More important than total count is velocity — getting new reviews consistently every month.
Does Local Pack ranking affect ChatGPT and AI recommendations?
Yes. The same signals that get you into the Local Pack — a complete GBP, consistent NAP data, recent reviews, and a clear website — are the same signals AI tools like ChatGPT use to decide which businesses to recommend. Ranking well in Local Pack significantly improves your AI search visibility.
